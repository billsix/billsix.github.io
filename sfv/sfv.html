SFV Deterministic Finite Automata


by Bill Six


In fighting games, a successful player is able to string together multiple attacks which, once started, the opponent is unable to defend. Since this string of attacks does much more damage to the opponent than a series of stray hits (i.e. one attack followed by a pause, thus allowing the opponent either to block or to counter-attack), a player who knows combos well is at a significant advantage over one who does not.

How does one interested in combos learn them?

Mainly, combos are put into lists; but there is a problem with that approach. Each character has many combos, many of which have common substrings. To list out every combo would have too much repetition to be reasonable. Experienced players can look at a long combo and instinctively deduce sub-combos which can be used in a different context (i.e., a listed combo that start with a jumping HP followed by standing MP, can also just start at standing MP).

But how does a novice who lacks experience learn from these condense lists?

Here, I attempt alleviate some of the aforementioned problems for the novice and experienced player alike by representing combos using Deterministic Finite Automata. Nodes surrounded by squares represent starting states/distances. Nodes surrounded by circles represent moves, and arrows point to new moves which may be linked/canceled from the previous move. Nodes surrounded by two circles are "end states".
